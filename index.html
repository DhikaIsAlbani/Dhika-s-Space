Part 1: Introduction

FRANZ KAFKA &
THE METAMORPHOSIS

About the Author

Franz Kafka (1883–1924) was a German-speaking Jewish writer born in Prague, then part of the Austro-Hungarian Empire. He worked as an insurance clerk by day, writing his surreal, existential masterpieces at night.

His writing explores themes of alienation, guilt, and the absurdity of modern bureaucracy—experiences drawn from his own strained relationship with his authoritarian father and his tedious office job.

The Novella

Written in 1912 and published in 1915, *The Metamorphosis* is one of the few works Kafka published during his lifetime. The story opens with one of literature's most famous sentences:

"When Gregor Samsa woke up one morning from unsettling dreams, he found himself changed in his bed into a monstrous vermin."
  • Genre: Absurdist/Existential Literature
  • Setting: The Samsa family apartment
  • Protagonist: Gregor Samsa, traveling salesman
  • Central Conflict: Physical and psychological transformation
Kafka's manuscript of The Metamorphosis
Part 2: Chapter 1

THE TRANSFORMATION

  • Morning Revelation: Gregor wakes to find himself transformed into a giant insect (often translated as "vermin" or "bug")
  • Initial Panic: Rather than horror at his condition, his first worry is being late for work and losing his job
  • The Manager's Visit: His boss arrives to investigate his absence, threatening his employment
  • Failure to Communicate: Gregor discovers his voice has changed—his family cannot understand his speech
  • Forced Seclusion: After revealing himself, his father drives him back into his room with a cane and newspaper
Key Insight: Gregor's immediate concern for work over his physical transformation reveals how thoroughly his identity has been consumed by his role as breadwinner.
Gregor's room becomes his prison
Part 3: Chapter 2

GREGOR'S ISOLATION

Family Dynamics Shift

Grete becomes Gregor's primary caretaker, bringing him food. Initially she brings milk and bread (human food), but soon realizes he prefers rotten scraps—his tastes are changing along with his body.

  • Hidden Observer: Gregor hides under the couch to avoid frightening his sister
  • Furniture Removal: The family decides to clear his room to give him crawling space
  • The Picture: Gregor clings to a framed photo of a woman in furs—his last connection to humanity
  • The Apple Incident: His father throws apples at him; one lodges in his back, creating a festering wound
"He thought back on his family with deep emotion and love. His opinion that he must disappear was, if possible, even firmer than his sister's."
The growing distance between Gregor and his family
Part 4: Chapter 3

DECAY & ACCEPTANCE

The Final Decline

Months pass. The family takes in lodgers to survive financially. Gregor becomes a "thing" to them—neglected, starving, and slowly dying from his infected wound.

  • The Violin: Grete plays for the lodgers; Gregor is drawn out by the music, showing he retains his human appreciation for art
  • The Confrontation: The lodgers see Gregor and threaten to leave without paying
  • The Betrayal: Grete insists "we must try to get rid of it"—referring to her brother as an object
  • Death: Gregor returns to his room and dies quietly overnight, accepting that he is a burden
  • Relief: The family feels liberated; they take a day off work and plan for Grete's marriage
"His body was completely flat and dry" — Kafka's detached description of Gregor's death
Part 5: Analysis

MAJOR THEMES

Alienation & Isolation

Gregor's physical transformation literalizes the emotional alienation he already felt as a traveling salesman. His family locks him away, and even his sister eventually rejects him.

The Absurdity of Life

Kafka presents the impossible transformation as mundane fact. No explanation is given for why Gregor changes, reflecting life's random cruelty and lack of inherent meaning.

Economic Dehumanization

Gregor is valued only for his earning potential. Once he cannot work, he becomes disposable. The story critiques how capitalism reduces human beings to their economic function.

Family & Conditional Love

The Samsas' love proves conditional on Gregor's utility. His self-sacrifice (working to pay off their debts) is forgotten once he becomes a burden.

Guilt & Self-Sacrifice

Even as an insect, Gregor worries about inconveniencing his family. His acceptance of death is the ultimate self-sacrifice—removing himself to ease their burden.

Identity Crisis

Who is Gregor if not the provider? His struggle to retain his humanity while his body changes questions what fundamentally makes us human.

Part 6: Symbols

KEY SYMBOLISM

🪲

The Insect

Represents Gregor's feelings of worthlessness and his low status within his family. Also symbolizes the "vermin" of the working class in modern society.

🍎

The Apple

Biblical allusion to the Fall of Man. The apple lodged in Gregor's back becomes a festering wound, symbolizing the family's rejection and original sin of filial betrayal.

🖼️

The Woman in Furs

The picture Gregor clings to represents his suppressed desires for beauty, sensuality, and escape from his dreary existence.

🚪

The Bedroom Door

Symbolizes the barrier between Gregor and humanity. When open, hope remains; when closed, complete isolation.

👔

The Father's Uniform

Represents the father's return to workforce vitality after Gregor's incapacitation, but also his rigidity and aggression.

🎻

The Violin

Art and beauty that Gregor can still appreciate even in his degraded state, representing the persistence of the human soul.

Part 7: Conclusion

FINAL THOUGHTS

The Kafkaesque Legacy

The term "Kafkaesque" has entered our vocabulary to describe surreal, disorienting situations where bureaucracy, absurdity, and powerlessness combine. *The Metamorphosis* remains the definitive example.

Modern Relevance: In an age of economic anxiety and isolation, Gregor's story resonates more than ever. Who hasn't felt reduced to a "bug" by their job? Who doesn't fear being discarded when no longer "useful"?

Key Takeaways

  • Identity is fragile and often externally defined
  • Empathy has limits when tested by inconvenience
  • The absurdity of existence requires no explanation
  • Self-worth should not depend on productivity
"The Metamorphosis is not about insects. It's about what happens when life pulls the rug out from under you. It's about feeling invisible in your own home."
Kafka's masterpiece continues to transform readers 110 years later
